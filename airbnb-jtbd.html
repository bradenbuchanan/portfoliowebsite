Foreward

This is a personal project I wanted to do to practice with my understand of the Jobs-to-be-Done methodology. I made up a situation and context below. This project assumes the reader has some familiarity with Jobs-to-be-Done and Outcome-Driven Innovation.

Context

  • Airbnb has faced challenges in recent years due to the COVID-19 pandemic, which severely impacted the travel industry.
  • As a result, the company has had to adapt its business strategy and find new ways to grow profitability in the travel space.
  • One way Airbnb is trying to achieve this is by uncovering new opportunities in the travel industry.
  • This could involve expanding into new markets or verticals, developing new products or services, or exploring new partnerships and collaborations.
  • With this in mind, there is a need to understand the key unmet needs within segments of users planning a vacation.

Objectives

  • Identify key unmet needs of users planning a vacation.
  • Uncover new opportunities for Airbnb in the travel industry.
  • Develop new products or services that meet the needs of travelers.
  • Explore new partnerships and collaborations in the travel industry.

Research approach

For this research I followed the Jobs-to-be-Done approach to understand what are the underlying needs of travel planners.


Below are the steps I took using the JTBD approach.

  • Define the market through a JTBD lens
  • Define the core job
  • Create a job map
  • Uncover outcome statements based on the core job
  • Quantification of the outcome statements
  • Outcome-based segmentation
  • Create opportunity landscape based on quantification of needs

Define the market through a JTBD lens

There are typically 3 main "customers" when looking at our solution through the JTBD lens.


  1. The job executor: this is the person using the product to get the core functional job done.

  2. The product lifecycle support team: this consists of the varying groups of people who support the product throughout its lifecycle. This includes people who install, transport, repair, maintain, upgrade or dispose of the product. They execute consumption chain jobs.

  3. The buyer: those who are responsible for making the financial purchase decision.

Since we are interested in understanding the core job and all the outcome statements for the primary executor of Airbnb (e.g., the end buyer), we will focus on the individuals who book Airbnbs and not those who list on Airbnb.

Define the core job

Determining the functional core job for any JTBD projects is the most tricky part of starting a project. Ultimately, we are interested in what our job executor is trying to accomplish using our product/service.


Depending on the goals of the research, we can determine the flight level of our core job.

  1. lower flight level (e.g., closer to the product): Will generate outcome statements that are closet to our product and service. Choosing a functional core job at this level would be great for understanding new generation product improvements.

  2. Higher flight level (e.g., farther away from the product): Will generate outcome statements that are farther to our product and service. Choosing a functional core job at this level would be great for looking outside of our solution into totally new areas. For example, if Airbnb used a JTBD study to explore the functional core job of "Plan a vacation", this would uncover outcome statements (e.g., needs) from a wide variety of solution spaces vs a core job that is at a lower flight level.


With the above in mind, I chose to go for the functional core job of: Plan a vacation

Create a job map

To create the job map, i interviewed 11 participants from UserTesting to create the job map and uncover outcomes related to the functional core job of planning a vacation.


These interviews helped uncover the ideal sequence of steps traveler planners need when planning a vacation. I learned this job can be broken down into 9 chronological steps that each have their own unique set of outcome statements.


Some interview questions I used to help create the job map are below...

  1. What needs to be defined or planned before planning a vacation?
  2. What needs to be located or gathereed before planning a vacation?
  3. What needs to be prepared or set up before planning a vacation?
  4. What needs to be confirmed before planning a vacation?

Uncover outcome statements based on the core job

Throughout the 11 qualitative interviews, I actively listened and documented every pain point, frustration, or annoyance that participants expressed when attempting to plan a vacation.

After each interview, I went through my notes and translated each pain, frustration, etc. into desired outcome statements.

For example, if I heard...
"I wish I knew I needed my covid vaccine printed out to get my airline ticket."

I would translate this to...
Minimize the time it takes to determine if travel documents are required before travel.

At the end of 11 qualitative interviews, I ended up with 115 desired outcome statements.

Quantification of the outcome statements

After compiling a list of outcome statements and complexity factors (e.g., situations, reasons, moments to why it is more difficult to perform the core job than others), I created a google form to hold my survey.

In an ideal world, I would use a platform like Qualtrics or a fielding agency to conduct the survey to ensure 1. higher data quality and 2. more customization with the survey.

Survey was sent out using a mixture of leftover UserTesting credits and online forums.

I stopped collecting survey data after it reached a sample size of n=310.

After combining the data to one excel file, I first cleaned the data in excel by removing any straightliners (SD SAT <.25) and by noticing patterns in how participants filled out the survey (conditional formatting in excel).

Next step is to calculate the opportunity score for each outcome statement and create a total market opportunity landscape.

Create opportunity landscape based on quantification of needs

Once the data has been cleaned, I calculated the opportunity score for each outcome statement and plotted the data based on the opportunity landscape.

Visually we can see that most of the outcome statements are in the approximately served and underserved areas, while few of the needs are in the overserved area.

There are 27 underserved needs from a total market perspective

These 27 underserved needs can then be clustered together based on their underlying themes. We can see in the image to the right which themes have the highest opportunity score, mean score, and number of opportunities.

In the executive report here, teams could go in and view the underlying outcome statements for each theme to see which outcomes have the highest opportunity to be addressed.

Outcome-based Segmentation

Outcome-based market segmentation is a process of identifying groups of customers with different unmet needs based on their desired outcomes. This approach focuses on understanding the "job" that the customer is trying to accomplish and uses their unmet desired outcomes as the basis for segmentation.

Below are the steps to segment the market based on outcomes.

I did this segmentation in RStudio. After the cluster analysis, I exported each cluster ID and put them back in my excel file to create opportunity landscapes for each segment. I tried 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 clusters but based on the outcomes and profiling data, the 3 segment cluster made the most sense based on the data.

In the end there were 3 clear segments based on the data. The Leisure Traveler, the Efficient Traveler, and the Budget-Savvy Traveler. Each segment has a variety of unmet needs, varying segment size, differing demographics, and areas of opportunities for a company like Airbnb to tackle.

Leisure Traveler

  • Has 20 underserved needs
  • Segment size = 40%
  • This segments biggest unmet needs are around financial needs and the burden when purchasing accommodations for the vacation
  • Higher likelihood to be from the US
  • Higher likelihood to be 30-49 years old

    • Efficient Traveler

  • Has 34 underserved needs
  • Segment size = 43%
  • This segment struggles most determining travel arrangements determining what activities to do on the vacation
  • From a variety of countries
  • Equal distribution across ages

    • Budget-Savvy Traveler

  • Has 30 underserved needs
  • Segment size = 16%
  • This segment struggles the most with social/emotional needs and determining the travel arrangements when planning a vacation
  • Higher likelihood to be from the US
  • Equal distribution across ages
